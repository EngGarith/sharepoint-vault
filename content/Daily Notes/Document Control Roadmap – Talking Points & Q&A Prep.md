**Document Control Roadmap – Talking Points & Q&A Prep**

---

**How to Use This Document**

This canvas is a **presenter’s aid**, not a policy document.

Use it to:

- Refresh intent before reviews or workshops
- Answer predictable questions consistently
- Keep explanations aligned with the roadmap language
- Avoid re-litigating decisions already made

If a question can be answered by quoting or paraphrasing this document, **do that instead of improvising**.

---

**Core Framing Talking Points (Use Early)**

**What This Is**

- A **sequenced activation of document authority**, not a list of new documents
- A way to stop document degradation **before** attempting full reconciliation
- An owner/operator model, not a project deliverables checklist

**What This Is Not**

- Not a promise of immediate accuracy
- Not a request for field walkdowns up front
- Not a document cleanup initiative
- Not an EPC best practice wish list

---

**The Single Most Important Distinction**

**Section 3 defines obligation. The phased roadmap defines capacity.**

- Section 3 answers: _“What do we ultimately own as an operator?”_
- The phases answer: _“When can we realistically govern each class of record?”_

These are intentionally not the same list.

---

**Frequently Asked Questions (With Approved Answers)**

**Q1: Do Phase 1 documents need to be fully as‑built before we control them?**

**Answer:** No.

Phase 1 is about **control to stop degradation**, not full reconciliation.

We explicitly recognize three states:

- Uncontrolled
- Controlled but known‑imperfect
- Reconciled / as‑built

Phase 1 moves documents from _uncontrolled_ to _controlled but known‑imperfect_.

Requiring full as‑built before control is explicitly rejected.

---

**Q2: Are we blessing bad drawings by controlling them?**

**Answer:** No. We are making known gaps visible instead of letting them drift silently.

Uncontrolled accuracy is riskier than controlled imperfection with explicit limitations.

---

**Q3: Why aren’t all documents in Section 3 included in Phase 1 or Phase 2?**

**Answer:** Because phases activate **authority**, not awareness.

All documents in Section 3 already exist as owner obligations.

Phases define when we can:

- assign a real owner
- enforce revision rules
- prevent silent degradation

Including everything early would be dishonest about staffing and governance capacity.

---

**Q4: When do the “other” documents come into play?**

**Answer:** They already exist today. What changes over time is **formal governance**, not existence.

Documents enter formal control when one or more of the following triggers occur:

- Risk trigger (safety or compliance impact)
- Reuse trigger (repeated project or operational use)
- Governance trigger (clear ownership and review cadence exists)

---

**Q5: Why are equipment lists, line lists, and instrument indexes in Phase 2 instead of Phase 1?**

**Answer:** Because lists demand higher discipline and manpower than drawings.

A half‑governed list erodes trust faster than a controlled drawing with gaps.

Phase 2 introduces register authority **after** baseline control is established.

---

**Q6: Why are plot plans, GAs, control narratives, and Cause & Effect in Phase 1?**

**Answer:** Because they maximize **operational legibility** with relatively low governance cost.

They are:

- heavily used by Operations and Maintenance
- critical for brownfield work and incident response
- easier to stabilize early

They help Ops understand _what exists_ and _how it behaves_ immediately.

---

**Q7: Are Brownfield Interface Registers and Tie‑In Registers evergreen documents?**

**Answer:** No.

They are **project‑authoritative, time‑bounded artifacts**.

They must be:

- authoritative during change
- reconciled at closeout
- retained for traceability

They are not maintained as evergreen records.

---

**Q8: Who owns Safe Operating Limits?**

**Answer:** Operations owns declared Safe Operating Limits.

Engineering provides the technical basis. EHS validates the hazard logic.

SOLs are operational commitments, not engineering maxima.

---

**Q9: Do Safe Operating Limits and Process Safety Time Assumptions need to be addressed in MOC?**

**Answer:** Yes.

MOC explicitly asks whether a change affects:

- declared Safe Operating Limits
- assumed response or escalation times

If those assumptions change, they must be reviewed and re‑declared.

---

**Q10: Why are action tracking and revalidation records owned by EHS instead of Operations?**

**Answer:** Because they exist to preserve regulatory continuity and risk memory.

Operations executes actions. Engineering designs solutions. EHS governs the system so actions do not die when projects or people move on.

---

**Q11: Why are emergency response plans and environmental permits marked as EHS‑owned?**

**Answer:** Because they exist due to regulatory obligation, not daily operational convenience.

Ops trains to them and executes them. EHS governs their accuracy, currency, and audit readiness.

---

**Q12: Does this mean Engineering is done once documents are handed over?**

**Answer:** No.

Engineering continues to:

- provide technical input
- support MOC
- update records when changes occur

What changes is **authority**, not participation.

---

**Phrases Worth Repeating Verbatim**

Use these consistently to avoid re‑explaining concepts:

- “Control comes before reconciliation.”
- “Authority is asserted over the best known truth.”
- “Phases activate governance, not awareness.”
- “Project artifacts collapse into evergreen records at closeout.”
- “Uncontrolled accuracy is riskier than controlled imperfection.”

---

**Red‑Flag Statements to Correct Immediately**

If you hear these, course‑correct:

- “We’ll wait until it’s perfect before controlling it.”
- “That’s just an engineering drawing.”
- “Ops doesn’t need to see that yet.”
- “We’ve always done it this way.”

Each of these undermines the roadmap’s core logic.

---

**Final Reminder for the Presenter**

You are not selling a tool. You are not promising accuracy. You are not assigning blame.

You are establishing **when the organization is willing to tell the truth about what it knows**.

That framing will carry you through almost every objection.
